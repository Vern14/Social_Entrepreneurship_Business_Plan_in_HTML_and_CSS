I. Executive Summary

Life's Little Adventures Farm is a 501(c)3 located in Wayne County, Ohio. Currently this organization provides equine therapy to children and families and rescues and rehabilitates animals. Life's Little Adventures Farm is looking to open a sensory integration clinic as another service for the community. Currently, many children who would benefit from sensory integration do not have the opportunity afforded to them. Sensory integration is not covered by most insurance companies and as a result is too expensive for families to afford.

The creation of a sensory integration clinic under Life's Little Adventures farm would provide a solution to this problem. The clinic will provide families with a way to receive affordable sensory integration therapy. The purpose of this plan is to provide information on the market demand for sensory integration, provide options for how the clinic will run and operate, and make financial projections about the future of the clinic once it is open.

We have found that there is large population of children in Wayne County who could benefit from this service. Our analysis was focused on children with ADHD because sensory integration has been found to be beneficial for this population. In Wayne County there are 1,400 children with ADHD. Based on this finding there is a large client base in Wayne County and the majority of these children are not receiving any form of sensory integration therapy at the moment. This clinic will provide a hurting population with a treatment that could have a large impact on their everyday lives.

The sensory integration clinic will be run as a co-operative. Members of the co-operative will pay a monthly fee and volunteer for the clinic every month. The goal of the co-operative organization is to encourage members to properly use and care for the equipment. By having members invest their time in the clinic we hope they will feel a sense of ownership and be motivated to take advantage of the services. The parents and guardians will play an active role in providing therapy for their child. Parents and guardians will be trained on how to use the equipment by an occupational therapist but will be responsible for implementing therapeutic plans and working with their child. When families go to use the clinic there will not be an occupational therapist on site. Families that sign up to be members of the clinic will be highly motivated and willing to put time into their child’s therapy.

Based on the risk analyses we performed and various scenarios that we examined (see the appendix for a full list of these scenarios), we have determined that the ideal scenario is for the clinic to be built on the farm without a paid employee. This will require total start-up funds of $31,020 to run through the first year, and will incur $3,420 in operating costs for each subsequent year. Based on an estimate of 21 families paying $30/month to use the clinic, it will break even in the 8th year of operations.


II. Organizational Plan
  • Business Summary


    • Mission

    The mission of Life's Little Adventures Farm is "a place where all children, especially those that are hurting, can visit the farm, have fun and be ministered to as well. The farm rescues animals and uses them to help children with physical and emotional healing. In turn, providing healing for hurting families. The farm facilitates Equine Play and also place animals so persons can do their own therapeutic riding.

    The sensory integration clinic will have a separate mission statement from the farm. This is because animals will not be a part of the service. Both mission statements share the same goal, to help hurting and healing children. The way in which this goal is achieved will be different for the clinic and farm. The mission for the clinic is to provide physical and emotional healing for children with sensory spectrum issues and to have families play an active role in the healing process.

    Background Information

    Life's Little Adventures Farm was incorporated as a 501C3 in 2002. Since then, Life's Little Adventures Farm has helped many children and families in Wayne County. The main achievements of Life's Little Adventures Farm in 2011 are summarized below:
    • 148 families dealing with loss of their home and possessions were helped
    • 16 "at risk teens" spent 40-120 hours doing equine assisted programming
    • 529 families participated in animal assisted programming


    These are the highlights of how Life's Little Adventures farm helped the Wayne County community in 2011. In 2011 Life's Little Adventures Farm had total revenue of $68,091. They are financially solvent and running a surplus.

    The current project is to create a sensory integration clinic through Life's Little Adventures Farm. Sensory integration is a form of occupational therapy that focuses on improving the senses of touch, space, and balance. By focusing on these three areas, mental and physical functions can be improved.

    This would be a beneficial addition to Life's Little Adventure Farm and the Wayne County community. Research indicates that EJ Therapy is the only other organization offering sensory integration in Wooster.In an interview with Eunice Herman, an occupational therapist working for EJ Therapy, it was stated that there is a client population for sensory integration and this clinic would be beneficial to the community. Eunice Herman shared that there are many families in Wayne County who cannot afford to consistently use the services offered by EJ Therapy and a more affordable option would help these clients.4 Not very many insurance companies will cover the cost of sensory integration and it is the families without insurance coverage that struggle to afford the service. Providing a cheaper alternative is one example of the strengths of this project but there are other pros and cons of the project that need to be discussed.

    Business Model

    The sensory integration clinic will run as a co-operative. The members of the co-operative will pay a monthly fee as much as $30 and volunteer a certain number of hours for the clinic. The monthly fee of $30 was calculated through an income statement. For more details see income statement in appendix. $30 is the most a client would pay and at this price the client would not be volunteering less than three hours each month for the clinic. Members who volunteer in the clinic will have their fee reduced based on the number of volunteer hours each month. If a member volunteers 8-10 hours for the clinic in a month their fee will be $20 that month. More volunteer hours means a reduced monthly fee.

    Running the clinic based on volunteers will be successful based on volunteer statistics in Ohio. Ohio ranks 21 out of the 50 states for number of volunteers. Since 2003 Ohio has been consistently above the national average for volunteer hours per resident. In 2010 the National average of volunteer hours per resident in the United States was 29.8 and Ohio had an average of 33.9. Ohio is also above the national average for providing labor for volunteering. .

    In order to determine what volunteer jobs are available to members a list of jobs will be created. Members will look at the list and decide which duties they would like to be responsible for. One of these jobs will be scheduling member and volunteer visits to the clinic. This job will include either calling or emailing members to schedule their visits. For members who have access to Internet scheduling will be done on an online calendar such as Google calendar and this will make the job easier.

    Life's Little Adventures Farm has been successful in having people volunteer, so having volunteers for the clinic will not be too much of a challenge. This is referred to as an opportunity in the SWOT analysis. In 2011 a home-schooled group and two church groups came out to volunteer.6 Based on volunteer statistics in Ohio 33.7% of volunteers volunteer their time to religious organizations.

    This means that Life's Little Adventures has the advantage of accessing volunteers through religious organizations. If more volunteers are needed beyond co-operative members Life’s Little Adventures Farm has potential volunteers in place.


  • Products and Services



    • The clinic will provide training for clients. At least once a month occupational therapists will meet with clients to go over how to setup, use, and care for the equipment. Clients will also learn what exercises will best benefit their child and how to advance the exercises when their child begins to show progress.

    Another service provided by the clinic will be equipment. Some of the equipment in the room may be small items families have in their own homes but there will also be unique products that families cannot afford on their own. A detailed list of some of the items that will be included in the clinic can be found in the appendix.

    One example of the equipment in the clinic is The Listening Program. This is a computer program used in many Cleveland school. It trains the auditory system to accurately process sound8. This will be useful in the clinic because when auditory perception is distorted academic, emotional, cognitive, and social problems are more likely to occur. This program is one example of the equipment that will be available for clients.

    The clinic will obtain equipment through donations, directly from manufacturers, and recycled materials. One way in which donations will be obtained is through the website gofundme.com. This is a site that allows people to raise money online. The first step is to create a donation website. Once the website is created the link can be shared and donations can be collected. Gofundme.com deducts a 5% fee from each donation and if no donations are received no fee is charged. This website will be used to target members of Wayne County but can also be spread to anyone with interest in this project. Sensory integration equipment can be expensive so any materials that can be donated or purchased at a reduced price will be helpful for this project. Equipment in the clinic will all be inspected before installation to ensure it will provide a benefit to the clients.

    Clients will be trained on how to use and care for the equipment so they can properly use it on their own. This training will be provided by volunteer occupational therapists. Training sessions will take place at least once a month. After going through training parents and guardians will be allowed to work one on one with their child to provide therapy. Parents and guardians will have a very hand on role in their child's therapy.


  • Administration



    • There are two options for the administration of the clinic: with a paid employee and without a paid employee. The advantage of not having a paid employee is a reduction in cost. The advantage of having a paid employee is the reliability that jobs around the clinic will be completed in a timely manner.

    With a Paid Employee

    The sensory integration clinic will be a part of Life's Little Adventures Farm and run under similar management. One part-time (20 hour week) administrator will be hired to keep the clinic running smoothly. This person will be responsible for scheduling families, making sure all of the clients are doing their part in the clinic, and handling any issues that arise. This job will be important during the start-up but it is possible that once the co-operative is running this position can be removed.

    Without a Paid Employee

    If it is decided that the clinic will run without a paid employee, then an operating committee will handle the day-to-day operations. The operating committee will consist of at least three people. This committee will be composed of outside volunteers and members of the co-operative. Melissa Willard will select members of the operating committee.

    Melissa Willard, who is Life's Little Adventures Farm owner and treasurer, will manage the financials of the clinic. She will be responsible for the monthly payments from clients, paying expenses, and dealing with any financial issues that arise.

    The occupational therapists that will provide training to families will all be volunteers at least once a month. At this time we have one confirmed occupational therapist. We hope to have between 3 and 5 volunteer occupational therapists. They will be recruited from Wayne County. Patti Andrich, MA, OTR/L, COVT has already volunteered to be one of the volunteer occupational therapists.

    The clinic will run in a co-operative style. Because it is organized like a co-operative members will be responsible for volunteering in the clinic. This is an opportunity of the current plan. This will cut down on the number of hired workers that will be needed. Another reason for the cooperative structure is so members feel like they have some ownership. This ownership should encourage members to clean up after themselves and respect the equipment.

    Location

    There are two options for the location of the clinic. The first option is on Life's Little Adventures Farm on Burbank Road in Wooster. Two shipping containers will be purchased and renovated for the site of the clinic. The advantages of this location is that it can be added onto Life's Little Adventures is it can be observed and overseen by the administration of Life's Little Adventures Farm and once the clinic is in setup there are no monthly rental fees.

    The second option for the location of the clinic is in a leased space closer to Wooster. The advantages of this option are a lower start-up cost and it is more accessible to clients who may not have their own transportation.

    Legal

    In order to keep insurance prices low and avoid any legal ramifications a fence will need to be built between the farm and clinic. All clients will be required to fill out a volunteer form and once this has been done they will be covered by the current insurance of Life's Little Adventures Farm.




III. Marketing Plan
  • Market Analysis


    • Our target market consists of the residents of Wayne County, Ohio aged 10-19 suffering from developmental or behavioral disorders who, due to economic hardship and lack of quality health insurance, cannot afford sensory integration therapy. To gain an approximation of the size of this market, we will examine publicly available statistics regarding one of the most common developmental disorders in the United States, Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD). From our research we have found that most of the children who have Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) are also potential candidates in need of sensory Integration (SI) Therapy. According to the National institute of Mental Health, the lifetime prevalence for Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) in 13 to 18 year old children, expressed as a percentage, is 9%. Census data indicates that there are 16,000 children aged 10-19 in Wayne County. By applying the nationwide average of children with ADHD to the number of children in Wayne County, we estimate that the number of children with ADHD in Wayne County who could benefit from sensory integration therapy is 1,400 (9% x 16,000).

    According to the 2007 data for age ranges 5 to 16, the total number of children with a mental disability in Wayne county Ohio is 731. This census data came from a table that represented mental disability by sex, age, and poverty status for the civilian non-institutionalized population 5 years and over. The 2009 annual report of the Mental Health and Recovery Board of Wayne and Holmes counties indicated that the board helped 1,200 children and teens receiving treatment for mental illness, emotional problems, substance abuse and addiction in both Holmes and Wayne County. According to the article "Demand for Mental Health Services Increases During the Economic Downturn" found on the Ohio department of mental health website, the demand for mental health services has increased even with the economic downturn.

    While these very broad statistics do not perfectly capture the exact market that we will be targeting, we believe that these broad estimations, together with our interactions with local community leaders, provides a clear indication that enough demand for this clinic exists to sustain our ideal membership level 20 families.

    Analysis of Competition

    The clinic will compete with several alternatives in the Wooster community in the form of other treatments for developmental disorders and other sources of sensory integration therapy. The alternative treatments include:

    • Traditional medications
    • Occupational therapy

    There is another sensory integration clinic in Wooster located in the Gault Family Learning Center. However, this clinic targets higher-income families with better insurance benefits that cover sensory integration. Our clinic will target lower-income families who lack the high quality insurance that is required to cover sensory integration therapy.


  • Marketing Strategy



    • The marketing strategy for the sensory integration clinic will have 4 aspects: online, roadside, newspaper, and pamphlets/newsletters.

    Online

    The clinic's online marketing presence will begin with a new tab on the current Life's Little Adventures Farm web-site. This tab will explain the purpose of the clinic, how people can get involved, contact information, and any other information that is deemed pertinent. Once funds are available, an entirely separate web-site will be created for the clinic. The clinic will also have a Facebook page where people can find contact information and be redirected to the website. A member of the operating committee will be in charge of maintaining the page by posting important announcements, such as upcoming OT visits, and any other necessary updates.

    Roadside

    The roadside advertising for the clinic will be painted directly onto the shipping containers in the form of a decorative mural. This mural will be visible to people driving by on interstate highway 83, and will feature the name of the clinic as well as the clinics web-site.

    Newspaper

    Prior to its grand opening, the clinic will take out advertisements in The Daily Record announcing its opening and contact information. The clinic may also be able to get the Daily Record to write an article on its opening, as this has been done for LLAF in the past.

    Newsletters/Pamphlets

    The clinic will announce its opening on the current e-mail delivered Life's Little Adventures newsletter, and periodic important announcements will also go out in this form until the clinic has its own established mailing list, at which point it will have its own separate newsletter. Pamphlets featuring a picture of the clinic, an explanation of what its purpose, and contact information will be distributed to local schools, doctor's offices, and community centers.


  • Public Relations



    • One of the chief sources of advertising for the clinic will be word-of-mouth recommendations, so a good relationship with the surrounding community of Wooster and Wayne County will be essential to the clinic's operation. The public will interact with the clinic through three channels: online, roadside, and pamphlets.

    Online:

    The clinic will have an online presence in the form of its website, as well as a separate Facebook page.

    Roadside:

    The clinic will be visible from interstate highway 83, and its name and web address will be visible to any cars that pass by.

    Pamphlets:

    The clinic will distribute pamphlets with a basic statement of its purpose, its web address, its street address and contact information.

    All three of these aspects of the clinic's public relations will be crafted with the goal of improving the clinic’s reputation in the community and bringing new families and donations to the clinic.


  • Customer Service


    • The clinic's website will feature a contact e-mail for members to contact if problems arise with the clinic. This e-mail account will be serviced by the secretary of the operating committee, who will serve as the intermediary between the clinic and the board of LLAF.
